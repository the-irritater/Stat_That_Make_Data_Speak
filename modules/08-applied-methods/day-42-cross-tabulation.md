# Day 42: Cross-Tabulation

*Comparing two categorical variables at once reveals patterns a single frequency table cannot.*

---

## Learning Objective

Understand how cross-tabulation (contingency tables) helps compare two categorical variables, and why it is the basis for chi-square tests.

---

## The Concept

A **cross-tabulation** (or contingency table) shows the frequency of combinations of two categorical variables.

|  | Online | Offline | Hybrid |
|--|--------|---------|--------|
| Statistics | 45 | 20 | 55 |
| Economics | 30 | 40 | 35 |
| CS | 50 | 15 | 60 |

Now we can answer: Do Statistics students prefer different learning modes than Economics students?

### Cross-tab vs frequency table:

- **Frequency table** → one variable at a time
- **Cross-tabulation** → two variables together

### Key uses:

- Survey analysis — compare demographics × preferences
- Market research — product × region
- Customer segmentation — age group × buying behavior
- Before chi-square test — check expected vs observed frequencies

---

## Real-World Example

A college compares department × learning preference. The cross-tab reveals CS students strongly prefer online/hybrid, while Economics students lean offline. This affects how the college allocates classroom vs online resources.

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaway

> Cross-tabulation compares two categorical variables and reveals relationships that single frequency tables miss. It is also the foundation for the chi-square test.
