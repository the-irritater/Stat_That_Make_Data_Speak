# Day 45: Scatter Plot

*Before you calculate a correlation, look at it. A scatter plot shows what numbers alone cannot.*

---

## Learning Objective

Understand how scatter plots reveal relationships between two numerical variables and why visual inspection is essential before statistical tests.

---

## The Concept

A **scatter plot** places one variable on the x-axis and another on the y-axis. Each point is one observation.

### What patterns reveal:

| Pattern | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Upward slope | Positive relationship | More study hours → higher marks |
| Downward slope | Negative relationship | More screen time → less sleep |
| No pattern | No linear relationship | Shoe size vs exam marks |
| Curved pattern | Non-linear relationship | Diminishing returns on study time |
| Clusters | Subgroups in data | Different customer segments |

### Why scatter plots matter before correlation:

Correlation coefficients can be misleading:
- Anscombe's quartet shows four datasets with the same correlation but completely different scatter patterns
- Outliers can inflate or deflate correlations
- Non-linear relationships have low correlation but strong patterns

Always plot before you calculate.

---

## Real-World Example

Study hours vs exam marks: points trend upward, but with high scatter. Students studying 3+ hours score ~18% higher on average, but some high-study students still score low. The scatter plot reveals that study time matters but is not the only factor.

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaway

> A scatter plot helps you see relationships before calculating them. Never skip the visual check — numbers alone can deceive.
