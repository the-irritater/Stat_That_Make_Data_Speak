# Day 50: Sampling Bias

*A bigger sample is not always a better sample. Representativeness matters more than size.*

---

## Learning Objective

Understand how sampling bias corrupts statistical conclusions and why representative sampling is essential.

---

## The Concept

**Sampling bias** occurs when the method of selecting a sample systematically excludes or over-represents certain groups.

### Common causes:

| Cause | Example | Problem |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Convenience sampling** | Surveying friends | Not representative |
| **Voluntary response** | Online review sites | Only extreme opinions |
| **Undercoverage** | Phone survey (excludes no-phone) | Missing demographics |
| **Non-response** | Low response rate | Respondents ≠ population |

### The Literary Digest Lesson (1936)

A magazine surveyed 2.4 million people and predicted Landon would defeat Roosevelt. Roosevelt won in a landslide. Why? The survey used telephone directories and magazine subscribers — overrepresenting wealthy voters during the Great Depression.

**2.4 million biased responses were less accurate than a representative sample of 50,000.**

### How to reduce sampling bias:

1. **Random sampling** — every member has equal chance
2. **Stratified sampling** — ensure all subgroups represented
3. **Systematic sampling** — select every nth individual
4. **Check demographics** — compare sample to known population statistics

---

## Real-World Example

A company wants to know customer satisfaction. They only survey customers who made purchases in the last week. This excludes churned customers, inactive users, and one-time buyers — all of whom may have very different opinions.

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaway

> A representative sample matters more than a large sample. Even millions of biased responses produce wrong conclusions. Always ask: who is NOT in this sample?
