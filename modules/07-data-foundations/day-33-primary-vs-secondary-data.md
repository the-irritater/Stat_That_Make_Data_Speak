# Day 33: Primary vs Secondary Data

*The data you collect yourself and the data someone else collected have different strengths — and different risks.*

---

## Learning Objective

Understand the difference between primary and secondary data sources, and why data provenance affects analysis quality.

---

## The Concept

**Primary data** is collected directly by the researcher for a specific purpose.

Examples: Google Form surveys, interviews, lab experiments, field observations.

**Secondary data** is data that already exists, collected by someone else for a different purpose.

Examples: Government reports, Kaggle datasets, company databases, published research, census data.

| Aspect | Primary Data | Secondary Data |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Collection | You collect it | Already available |
| Cost | Higher (time + effort) | Lower |
| Control | Full control over design | No control |
| Relevance | Tailored to your question | May not perfectly fit |
| Risk | Poor sampling | Outdated or mismatched |

### The Critical Question

Before analyzing any dataset, ask three questions:

1. **Who** collected this data?
2. **Why** was it collected?
3. **How** was it collected?

If you cannot answer these, you cannot fully trust the conclusions.

---

## Real-World Example

- **Primary**: You create a Google Form and survey 200 students about study habits → you control the questions, sample, and timing.
- **Secondary**: You download a student performance dataset from Kaggle → convenient, but you don't know how responses were collected or whether the sample was representative.

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaway

> Data quality depends not only on numbers, but also on how the data was collected. Always check the source before trusting the analysis.
