Day 56 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Normality Assumption

Normality assumption means the data, or sometimes the residuals, should be approximately normally distributed.

This does not always mean perfect normality.

Real data is rarely perfect.

Real-life example:

Student marks may roughly follow a bell-shaped pattern.

Most students score around the average.

Very low and very high marks are less common.

Data analytics use:

Normality is often checked before t-tests, ANOVA, regression diagnostics, and confidence intervals.

Common ways to check normality:

Histogram
Q-Q plot
Shapiro-Wilk test
Skewness and kurtosis

Key takeaway:

Normality is not about perfection.

It is about whether the data is close enough for the method to work reasonably.
