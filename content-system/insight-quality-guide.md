# Insight Quality Guide — Stats That Make Data Speak

## The Problem

Most beginner insights are too obvious to stand out:

> Students who study more perform better.

Nobody shares this. Nobody hires for this.

---

## The Standard

Every insight in a notebook or LinkedIn post must include at least one of:

### 1. A Number
> Students studying more than 3 hours scored around 18% higher on average.

### 2. A Comparison
> Customers who received the discount were 2.3× more likely to return within 30 days than those who did not.

### 3. A Surprise
> Despite spending 40% more on ads, the campaign group showed no statistically significant improvement in conversion (p = 0.42).

---

## Weak vs Strong Insights

| Weak ❌ | Strong ✅ |
|---------|----------|
| Salary varies by department | Engineering salaries are 35% higher than Marketing, but Marketing has 2× the variance |
| More screen time reduces productivity | Productivity drops sharply after 6 hours of screen time, but the relationship is flat below 4 hours |
| Discounts help sales | Discounts above 20% increase order volume but decrease profit margin — the optimal discount is 12–15% |
| Age affects spending | The 36–45 age group spends 40% more than any other segment, but has the lowest return rate |

---

## The Formula

```text
[Subject] + [specific finding] + [context or caveat]
```

Examples:
- "Students studying 3+ hours scored 18% higher, **but variation remained high**"
- "Mumbai delivery times averaged 3 days, **compared to 5.2 days in Delhi**"
- "The model explains 75% of variance, **but residuals show non-linearity in the ₹20–40 lakh range**"

---

## LinkedIn Post Quality Check

Before posting, verify:

- [ ] Does the insight have a specific number?
- [ ] Does it make a comparison (higher/lower, more/less, vs)?
- [ ] Would someone be surprised or learn something new?
- [ ] Does it help someone avoid a wrong decision?
- [ ] Is the chart shown inside the post (not just linked)?

If none of these are true, the insight is too generic.

---

## Daily Filter

```text
Does this show that I can think with data?

If not, improve it.
```
