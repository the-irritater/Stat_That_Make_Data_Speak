# Updated Execution System

## Core Rule

Every piece of work must answer one question:

**What decision does this analysis help someone make?**

If the answer is unclear, the notebook is not finished.

A notebook is not complete when the code runs. A notebook is complete when it has:

1. A real dataset.
2. One clear business or research question.
3. Cleaned data.
4. One strong visual.
5. One insight with a number, comparison, or surprise.
6. One decision-focused takeaway.
7. A LinkedIn post linked to GitHub.

---

## Revised Weekly Output Rule

Each week must produce:

* One strong applied notebook.
* One lighter analysis or mini notebook.
* One shareable visual.
* One LinkedIn post with the visual shown inside the post.
* One GitHub link.
* One decision-focused takeaway.

Every two weeks, create one full case study.

Week 1–2:

* Two notebooks per week.

After Week 2:

* One strong notebook plus one lighter analysis per week.

This keeps quality high and prevents burnout.

---

## Insight Quality Rule

A weak insight sounds like this:

> Students who study more perform better.

A stronger insight sounds like this:

> Students studying more than 3 hours scored around 18% higher on average, but the variation remained high. This suggests study time matters, but it is not the only factor affecting performance.

Every insight must include at least one of these:

* A number.
* A comparison.
* A surprise.

No number, no comparison, and no surprise usually means the insight is too obvious.

## Day 33 | Primary vs Secondary Data

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 33 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Primary vs Secondary Data

Primary data is collected directly by the researcher.

Secondary data is already collected by someone else.

Real-life example:

If you create a Google Form and collect responses from students, that is primary data.

If you use government reports, Kaggle datasets, company records, or published research data, that is secondary data.

Primary data gives control over questions and sample design.

Secondary data saves time and cost.

But both have risks.

Primary data may suffer from poor sampling.

Secondary data may not perfectly match your research objective.

Data analytics use:

Before analyzing any dataset, ask:

Who collected this data?
Why was it collected?
How was it collected?

Key takeaway:

Data quality depends not only on numbers, but also on how the data was collected.

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 36 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Missing values occur when some data points are not recorded.

Real-life example:

Some may forget to enter age.

Some may leave optional questions blank.

Missing values can affect averages, percentages, models, and conclusions.

Common ways to handle missing values:

Remove the missing rows.
Fill with mean, median, or mode.
Use domain knowledge.
Use statistical imputation methods.

But do not blindly fill missing values.

First ask why the value is missing.

Key takeaway:

Missing values are not just empty cells.

They are signals that need investigation.

## Day 47 | Simpson's Paradox

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 47 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's Paradox happens when a trend appears in separate groups but changes or reverses when groups are combined.

Sounds strange, but it happens in real data.

Real-life example:

A treatment may seem better for men and better for women separately.

But after combining both groups, the other treatment may appear better overall.

Why?

Because group sizes are different.

Data analytics use:

This matters in healthcare, education, business, marketing, and social research.

If we ignore groups, we may make the wrong conclusion.

Key takeaway:

Overall data can sometimes mislead.

Always check important subgroups before making decisions.

## Day 48 | Confounding Variable

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 48 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Confounding Variable

A confounding variable is a hidden factor that affects both the cause and the result.

It can make two variables look related even when the relationship is not direct.

Real-life example:

Ice cream sales and drowning cases may both increase together.

But ice cream does not cause drowning.

The hidden factor is summer.

Hot weather increases ice cream sales and swimming activity.

Data analytics use:

Confounding is important in research, regression, medical studies, marketing analysis, and social science.

Ignoring confounders can lead to wrong conclusions.

Key takeaway:

Before saying "X causes Y", check whether a third variable is influencing both.

Real-life example:

Suppose we want to study all Mumbai University students.

The sample is easy to collect, but it may not represent all students.

Key takeaway:

A bigger sample is not always a better sample.

A representative sample is better than a convenient sample.

## Day 52 | Margin of Error

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 52 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Margin of Error

Margin of error tells us how much a sample result may differ from the true population value.

Real-life example:

A survey says 60% of students prefer online learning, with a margin of error of ±4%.

This means the true percentage may be around 56% to 64%.

Why does this happen?

Because we usually study a sample, not the full population.

Different samples may give slightly different results.

Data analytics use:

Margin of error is useful in surveys, election polls, customer research, and public opinion studies.

Key takeaway:

Survey results are estimates, not exact truth.

Margin of error shows the uncertainty around the estimate.

## Day 53 | Statistical vs Practical Significance

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 53 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Statistical Significance vs Practical Significance

Statistical significance means the result is unlikely to be due to chance.

Practical significance means the result is actually useful in real life.

Real-life example:

A company changes a button color on its website.

Conversion rate increases from 10.00% to 10.05%.

With a very large sample, this may be statistically significant.

But is it practically useful?

Maybe not.

The increase may be too small to matter in business terms.

Data analytics use:

Do not stop at p-value.

Always ask:

Is the effect large enough to matter?

Key takeaway:

A result can be statistically significant and still practically useless.

Homogeneity of variance means different groups should have similar spread or variability.

Real-life example:

Suppose we compare exam marks across three teaching methods.

If one group has very stable marks and another group has highly scattered marks, the variances are different.

This can affect tests like ANOVA.

Data analytics use:

Homogeneity of variance is commonly checked before ANOVA and some t-tests.

Common tests include Levene's test and Bartlett's test.

But graphs also help.

Box plots can quickly show whether spreads look similar.

Key takeaway:

When comparing group means, do not only compare averages.

Also check whether the groups have similar variability.

Multicollinearity happens when predictor variables in a regression model are highly related to each other.

Real-life example:

Suppose we predict house price using:

These two variables may be strongly related.

Key takeaway:

When predictors are too similar, the model becomes harder to interpret.

## Day 59 | Residuals in Regression

### LinkedIn Caption

Day 59 of Stats That Make Data Speak

Topic: Residuals in Regression

A residual is the difference between the actual value and the predicted value.

Formula:

Residual = Actual value – Predicted value

Real-life example:

A model predicts a house price as ₹80 lakh.

The actual price is ₹85 lakh.

Residual = ₹85 lakh – ₹80 lakh = ₹5 lakh

Residuals help us check whether the regression model is working well.

Data analytics use:

Residual plots help detect:

Non-linearity
Outliers
Unequal variance
Poor model fit
Missing patterns

Key takeaway:

Regression does not end after getting an equation.

Residuals tell us whether the model can be trusted.
